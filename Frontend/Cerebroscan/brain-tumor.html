Understanding Brain Tumors

A brain tumor is a growth of abnormal cells in or around the brain. These tumors can be malignant (cancerous) or benign (non-cancerous) and may originate in the brain (primary) or spread from other parts of the body (secondary or metastatic).

Types of Brain Tumors

  • Glioma: Tumors that start in the glial cells of the brain or spine.
  • Meningioma: Usually benign tumors that form in the membranes covering the brain and spinal cord.
  • Pituitary Tumors: Growths in the pituitary gland that can affect hormone production.

Common Symptoms

  • Persistent headaches (especially worse in the morning)
  • Seizures
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Blurred or double vision
  • Speech difficulties
  • Memory loss or confusion
  • Changes in personality or behavior

Diagnosis Process

Brain tumors are diagnosed using neurological exams, imaging techniques like MRI or CT scans, and sometimes biopsy. Advanced AI tools like CerebroScan can assist in early detection by automatically classifying and segmenting tumors from MRI scans.

Treatment Options

  • Surgery: Removal of the tumor, often the first line of treatment.
  • Radiation Therapy: High-energy rays to destroy or shrink tumors.
  • Chemotherapy: Use of drugs to kill tumor cells.
  • Targeted Therapy: Drugs or substances that block cancer growth by targeting specific molecules.
  • AI-assisted Monitoring: Regular follow-up scans analyzed by AI to detect changes in tumor size or type.

Living with a Brain Tumor

Coping with a brain tumor requires medical, emotional, and lifestyle support. Patients are encouraged to maintain a healthy lifestyle, seek support from loved ones, and follow a personalized care plan from their medical team.
